## 1. Adaptation vs. Mitigation — Master Comparison

> 📌 **EXAM HIGH PRIORITY** — Appears in PYQ (Q6b), CT-II (Set 1, Q3), and Final Exam

> **Definition**: **Adaptation** = adjusting to climate change to reduce its harmful effects on human societies and natural systems.
> **Definition**: **Mitigation** = taking action to reduce GHG emissions and slow the rate of global warming.

**Master Comparison Table**:

| Feature | **ADAPTATION** | **MITIGATION** |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| **Core Question** | "How do we live with change?" | "How do we stop the change?" |
| **Addresses** | Consequences / impacts | Causes (GHG emissions) |
| **Timescale** | Short to medium term | Long term (decades to centuries) |
| **Geographic Scale** | Local, regional | Global |
| **Who benefits?** | The region/community taking action | The whole world |
| **Who pays?** | Local or national governments | All nations (ideally) |
| **Relationship** | Complementary | Complementary |
| **Examples** | Sea walls, drought-resistant crops, early warning systems, Miyawaki forests, heat action plans | Renewable energy, CCS, carbon trading, energy efficiency, biofuels, nuclear power |
| **IPCC definition** | Adjustment in natural/human systems to moderate harm or exploit beneficial opportunities | Human interventions to reduce the sources or enhance the sinks of GHGs |
| **Case Study example** | Coastal city builds sea walls (reduces impact of sea level rise) | Country shifts from coal to solar (reduces emissions) |

**Key Exam Point**: Adaptation and mitigation are **NOT alternatives** — the world needs both. Mitigation prevents future warming; adaptation manages unavoidable current warming. Over-relying on adaptation alone without mitigation leads to climate impacts that exceed adaptive capacity.

**Case Study Application**:
- **CT-II Set 1 Q1**: A coastal city building sea walls = **ADAPTATION** (reduces consequence of sea level rise; does not reduce emissions)
- **PYQ Q6b**: Decarbonization = **MITIGATION**; resilience building = **ADAPTATION**

## 3. Carbon Trading — Complete Exam Coverage

> 📌 **HIGH PRIORITY** — CT-II Set 2 Q2, Q3; covers Unit 4 content

**Definition Summary** (see also [_FCC_UNIT_4](\_FCC_UNIT_4.md)):
- **Carbon Credit**: A permit to emit **1 tonne CO₂ equivalent**
- **Carbon Trading**: Market-based system for buying/selling these permits
- **1 carbon credit = 1 permit = 1 tonne CO₂e**

**How Carbon Trading Helps Meet Emission Targets**:

> CT-II Set 2 Q3: "How does carbon trading help countries/companies meet their emission targets?"

**Answer Points**:
1. **Economic efficiency**: Countries or companies that can reduce emissions cheaply do so; those for whom it's expensive **buy permits** — global emission target still met but at minimum total cost
2. **Revenue generation**: Under-emitters earn revenue by selling surplus credits → incentivizes going beyond target
3. **Clear cap**: Total permits issued = the emission target → ensures the environmental limit is respected
4. **Flexibility**: Allows businesses time to transition technologies while still meeting legal obligations
5. **CDM effect**: Developed countries fund clean projects in developing countries → reduces global emissions while supporting development
6. **Price signal**: Carbon price signals investors and businesses to prefer low-carbon technologies → long-term structural shift

**Carbon Credit vs. Carbon Tax**:

| Feature | Carbon Trading (ETS) | Carbon Tax |
|---------|---------------------|------------|
| Quantity control | YES — cap is fixed | NO — quantity uncertain |
| Price certainty | NO — price fluctuates | YES — price fixed |
| Examples | EU ETS, Kyoto Protocol | Sweden carbon tax, BC Canada |
| Flexibility | High | Low (uniform price for all) |

## 4. Miyawaki Method — Complete Exam Coverage

> 📌 **HIGH PRIORITY** — Miyawaki is relevant to Units 5 AND 7; frequently appears in questions

**Complete Answer Framework**:

**Origin**: Developed by Japanese botanist **Dr. Akira Miyawaki** at Yokohama National University in the 1970s–80s. Based on the concept of **Potential Natural Vegetation (PNV)** — planting the species that would naturally grow at that location.

**The 5-Step Method**:

| Step | Action | Why |
|------|--------|-----|
| 1 | Soil preparation (loosen + add organic matter: rice husks, coconut shells, sawdust, straw) | Compacted urban soil cannot support deep roots; organic matter improves drainage and nutrients |
| 2 | Select 30–50+ **native multi-layered species** | Native species are adapted to local climate; multi-layering mimics natural forest structure |
| 3 | Dense planting: 3–5 plants/m² in random mixed arrangement | Competition encourages faster upward growth; mixed species prevents disease spread |
| 4 | Active management for 2–3 years: watering + weeding | Young plants vulnerable; weeds compete; no pesticides or fertilizers needed |
| 5 | Self-sustaining forest after 3 years; no further intervention | Leaf litter creates natural mulch; biodiversity colonizes naturally |

**Outcomes**:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Growth rate | **10× faster** than conventional plantation |
| Density | **30× denser** than conventional plantation |
| CO₂ absorption | **30× more** than monoculture forest |
| Time to mature forest | **20–30 years** (vs. 200 years naturally) |
| Self-sustaining from | Year **2–3** |
| Minimum land required | ~100 m² |
| Cost after 3 years | Near zero (self-sustaining) |

**Why Miyawaki for India**:
- Dense urban areas with small unused plots
- High biodiversity potential (tropical climate)
- Reduces urban heat island (critical given heat wave risk)
- NMMC has planted 100+ Miyawaki forests

**Exam Trick — Adaptation AND Mitigation roles**:

| Role | How |
|------|-----|
| **Mitigation** | Sequesters CO₂; absorbs 30× more carbon than monoculture |
| **Adaptation** | Reduces urban heat; flood mitigation; air quality improvement; biodiversity support |

## 8. CT-II Set 1 — March 2026

> **Date**: 23.03.2026 | **Duration**: 45 minutes | **Marks**: 10

**Q1 (2 marks, Compulsory)**: A coastal city builds sea walls to protect against rising sea levels. Is this adaptation or mitigation?

> **Answer**: This is **adaptation**.
> - Sea walls reduce the *vulnerability* to the *consequence* (sea level rise)
> - They do NOT reduce GHG emissions — they do not address the *cause* (climate change)
> - Adaptation definition: adjustments to reduce harm from climate impacts
> - Other adaptation examples: drought-resistant crops, early warning systems, Miyawaki forests

**Q2 (2 marks, Compulsory)**: Explain how melting polar ice accelerates warming using the albedo concept.

> **Answer**: This is the **ice-albedo positive feedback**:
> - Ice/snow has high albedo (~0.8–0.9) → reflects ~80–90% of solar radiation
> - As warming melts ice, darker ocean/land is exposed → albedo drops (~0.06 for ocean)
> - More solar energy absorbed → surface heats more → more ice melts → feedback continues
> - This is a **positive feedback** — amplifies the initial warming

**Q3 (3 marks)**: The 2°C target supports both mitigation and adaptation — explain.

> **Answer**:
> - **The 2°C target** (Paris Agreement) aims to limit global warming to well below 2°C above pre-industrial
> - It supports **mitigation** because limiting warming requires large, sustained reductions in GHG emissions → nations must decarbonize their economies, shift to renewables, improve efficiency
> - It supports **adaptation** because even under the 2°C scenario, significant impacts occur (sea level rise, extreme weather, biodiversity loss) — adaptation measures (sea walls, drought-resistant agriculture, heat action plans) are needed to manage these unavoidable impacts
> - Therefore: Mitigation strives to achieve the 2°C limit; adaptation manages the impacts that occur even if 2°C is achieved
> - Both are explicitly part of the Paris Agreement's objectives

**Q4 (3 marks)**: Objectives of Kyoto Protocol + Paris Agreement.

> **Kyoto Protocol Objectives**:
> - Reduce combined emissions of 6 GHGs (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, SF₆, HFCs, PFCs) by Annex I countries
> - Collective target: 5.2% below 1990 levels for 2008–2012 commitment period
> - Provide flexibility mechanisms (ET, JI, CDM) for cost-effective reductions
> **Paris Agreement Objectives**:
> - Well below 2°C temperature target, pursuing 1.5°C
> - Increase ability to adapt to climate impacts
> - Make finance flows consistent with low-GHG development
> - All parties submit NDCs (nationally determined contributions)

**Q5 (3 marks)**: Why are climate change targets necessary?

> - Without targets, countries have no accountability for emission reductions — "business as usual" leads to 3–4°C warming
> - Targets provide a **clear, shared goal** that coordinates international action (prevents free-rider problem)
> - Targets create **certainty for investors** — businesses and financial markets need long-term signals to shift investment from fossil fuels to clean energy
> - Targets help **vulnerable communities** by limiting the degree of warming they must adapt to
> - Scientific evidence shows impacts increase non-linearly — difference between 1.5°C and 2°C is significant (e.g., coral reef survival, heat extremes, sea level rise)
> - Targets motivate **technological innovation** — a clear price on carbon and emission limits drives R&D

## 9. CT-II Set 2 — March 2026

> **Date**: 23.03.2026 | **Duration**: 45 minutes | **Marks**: 10

**Q1 (2 marks, Compulsory)**: Why are cyclones not observed near the equator?

> **Answer**: Due to the **Coriolis force** being zero at the equator:
> - Cyclone formation requires the Coriolis force to initiate and sustain rotation
> - Coriolis force is perpendicular to motion, causes deflection — but at the equator, Coriolis = 0
> - Without Coriolis deflection, converging air flows directly toward pressure centers without spiraling
> - The minimum latitude for cyclone formation is approximately **5°** from the equator
> - Tropical cyclones form between 5° and 20° latitude

**Q2 (2 marks, Compulsory)**: Purpose of carbon trading + difference from carbon credits.

> **Carbon Credit**: A permit allowing the holder to emit **1 tonne of CO₂ equivalent**. It is a unit of measurement.
> **Carbon Trading**: The **market mechanism** where carbon credits are bought and sold. Companies/countries that exceed their allowed emissions buy credits from those who are below their limit.
> **Purpose of Carbon Trading**:
> - Puts a price on carbon emissions → economic incentive to reduce
> - Ensures total emissions stay within the set cap
> - Allows emission reductions to happen where they are most cost-effective globally

**Q3 (3 marks)**: How does carbon trading help meet emission targets?

> See [[#3. Carbon Trading — Complete Exam Coverage]] for full answer.

**Q4 (3 marks)**: A nation shifts from coal to solar — how does this reduce carbon emissions?

> - Coal combustion releases large amounts of CO₂ (~1 kg CO₂ per kWh for coal vs. near-zero for solar)
> - Switching to solar: no combustion → no CO₂ emissions during operation
> - **Direct reduction**: each kWh generated from solar replaces coal-generated kWh and its CO₂
> - **Indirect reduction**: less demand for coal mining → reduced methane (CH₄) leakage from mines; no combustion-related SO₂, NOₓ
> - **Carbon sink benefit**: if coal mine land is restored → vegetation grows → absorbs CO₂
> - **Long-term**: reduced energy import dependence; energy price stability; no carbon price risk
> - Over 25 years, a solar panel saves roughly 20–40 tonnes of CO₂ per kW installed

**Q5 (3 marks)**: Why is limiting warming to 1.5°C important?

> - **Scientific consensus (IPCC SR1.5)**: at 1.5°C warming vs. 2°C, significantly fewer people exposed to climate-related risks
> - **Coral reefs**: at 1.5°C, 70–90% of coral reefs are lost; at 2°C, virtually **ALL** (>99%) coral reefs lost → entire marine ecosystem impact
> - **Sea level rise**: at 1.5°C, 0.26–0.77 m rise by 2100; at 2°C, 0.30–0.93 m — millions fewer people displaced
> - **Food security**: 1.5°C limits crop yield reductions; at 2°C, maize, wheat, rice yields fall significantly more in tropical regions
> - **Extreme weather**: frequency and intensity of heat waves, floods, droughts all lower at 1.5°C
> - **Arctic sea ice**: at 1.5°C, ice-free Arctic summer expected once per century; at 2°C, once per decade
> - **Irreversibility**: above 2°C, risk of triggering tipping points (AMOC collapse, permafrost methane, ice sheet disintegration) that cause irreversible runaway warming
