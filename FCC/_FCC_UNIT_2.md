## 1. Introduction — Climate vs. Paleoclimate

> **Definition**: **Paleoclimatology** is the study of past climates using proxy data — preserved physical records — to understand how Earth's climate has changed over millions of years.

| Aspect | Climate | Paleoclimate |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| Data Source | Actual weather observations | Proxy / preserved physical data |
| Surface records | ~100–200 years | Millions of years |
| Upper air data | ~50 years | Ice core reconstructions |
| Ocean temperature | ~50 years | Sediment records |

---

## 2. Paleoclimate Proxies

> **Definition**: **Paleoclimate proxies** are preserved physical data from natural recorders of climate variability used to reconstruct past climates without direct measurements.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  PALEOCLIMATE PROXY SOURCES                  │
├──────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Ice Cores       │ Trapped gases, dust, isotopes in annual   │
│                  │ ice layers; each layer = 1 year           │
├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Tree Rings      │ Width = climate conditions that year      │
│  (Dendro)        │ Wide = warm/wet; Thin = drought           │
├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Coral Records   │ Annual growth bands; chemistry = temp     │
├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Ocean Sediments │ Foraminifera shells; pollen; isotopes     │
├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Borehole Temps  │ Subsurface temperature profiles           │
├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Pack Rat Middens│ Preserved plant material in rodent nests  │
├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Historical Data │ Ship logs, early weather observer records │
└──────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**What Ice Cores can measure**:
- Past atmospheric composition (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O concentrations)
- Past temperature (via oxygen isotope ratios — δ¹⁸O)
- Volcanic events (sulfate layers)
- Dust and aerosol loading

## 3. Tree Rings — Dendrochronology and Dendroclimatology

> **Definition**: **Dendrochronology** is the study of annual growth rings in trees to determine the **age** of a tree. Alternating light (early wood) and dark (late wood) bands in a cross-section; each band = 1 year.

> **Definition**: **Dendroclimatology** is the science of determining **past climates** from trees. Ring width and density reflect growing conditions for each year.

**Ring Interpretation**:

| Ring Characteristic | Climate Implication |
|--------------------|---------------------|
| Wide / thick ring | Warm, wet year — favorable growth conditions |
| Thin / narrow ring | Cold, dry year — drought or frost |
| Missing ring | Extremely harsh year (tree skipped growth) |

**Key Distinction**:

| Discipline | Purpose | Output |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Dendrochronology** | Determine **age** of a tree / wooden artefact | Timeline, calendar dating |
| **Dendroclimatology** | Reconstruct **past climate** conditions | Temperature/precipitation history |

> 📌 Dendroclimatology has become a **major tool** for reconstructing climates of the past **millennium** in many areas of the world.

## 4. Borehole Temperatures

> **Definition**: **Borehole thermometry** measures temperature profiles in holes drilled into the ground. The shape of the temperature-depth curve reflects how surface temperature has changed in the past — because heat diffuses slowly through rock.

**Key Features**:
- Temperature curves bend toward **higher temperatures near the surface** — reflecting response of bedrock to recent atmospheric warming
- Does **NOT** need to be calibrated against the instrumental record → **independent** paleoclimate record
- Used to validate other proxy techniques (cross-check with tree rings, ice cores, etc.)
- A global dataset of borehole temperatures closely matches the instrumental global temperature reconstruction

## 5. Polar Regions and Sea Level Rise

**Past Sea Level Evidence (IPCC)**:
- Global mean sea level exceeded **5 m above present** during warm periods when temperature was up to 2°C warmer than pre-industrial (very high confidence)
- Last interglacial period: sea level was at least **5–10 m higher** than present for several thousand years
- Major factors: Ocean thermal expansion + changes in glaciers and ice caps

**Recent Trends**:
- Late 19th / early 20th century: transition from low mean rates of rise to **higher rates** (high confidence)
- Ice sheets responsible for ~**one-third** of global sea level rise since 1993
- Current rise rate: ~**3.2 mm/year** (over 70 mm total since 1995)
- East Antarctica: shifting winds and westerly ocean currents pushing warm water toward ice sheet → accelerated thinning

## 6. Ice Ages

> **Definition**: An **Ice Age** is a period when global temperatures drop enough that glaciers advance and cover over one-third of Earth's surface both laterally and longitudinally. Within an ice age, a **glacial** is the cold advance phase; an **interglacial** is the warmer retreat phase.

**Prime Characteristics**:
- Formation of heavy ice layers covering major landmass portions
- Continental glaciations — freezing of rivers and freshwater bodies
- Drastic changes to flora and fauna; mass extinctions and new species evolution

**Four Causes of Ice Ages**:

| Cause | Mechanism |
|-------|-----------|
| **Atmospheric Changes** | Reduction in greenhouse gases → Earth cannot retain heat |
| **Continental Positions** | Tectonic movements can block warm ocean currents from equatorial to polar regions |
| **Oceanic Current Fluctuations** | Changes in current patterns alter heat distribution globally |
| **Tibetan Plateau** | Increased height from tectonic uplift passed the snow line → triggered Pleistocene Ice Age |

**Key Timeline Facts**:
- Last ice age ended: ~**8,000 years ago**
- Earth is currently in a **Quaternary Glaciation Stage** — intermittent period between full glacial and greenhouse
- The **Little Ice Age** (Middle Ages): likely caused by reduced sunspot activity; "frost fairs" on frozen River Thames

## 7. Interglacial Cycles

> **Definition**: An **interglacial** is the warmer period between ice ages where glaciers retreat and sea levels rise. A **glacial** is the colder period within an ice age where glaciers advance.

**Statistics over past 450,000 years**:
- Glacials last: **70,000–90,000 years**
- Interglacials last: ~**10,000 years**
- Glacials occur **7–9× more often** than interglacials

**Sea Level Changes**:
- During **glacial**: sea level drops ~**100 m** (water stored in growing ice sheets)
- During **interglacial**: sea level rises as ice melts (+ ocean thermal expansion)

**The Holocene Interglacial**:
- Started: ~**11,700 years ago** — currently ongoing (abnormally long)
- Key characteristics: stable climate, rising sea levels at start, development of agriculture and human civilizations

**Human Impact on Interglacial Duration**:
- A new glaciation was expected to begin, but **anthropogenic climate change is delaying it** by thousands to hundreds of thousands of years
- Holocene expected to last at least another **150,000 years** due to human-caused warming

**Glacial-Interglacial Sea Level Comparison**:

| Period | Sea Level Change | Cause |
|--------|-----------------|-------|
| Glacial | Drops ~100 m | Water evaporated and stored in growing glaciers |
| Interglacial | Rises | Ice sheets and glaciers melt; ocean heated and expands |
| Current (Anthropogenic) | Rising 3.2 mm/yr | Accelerated ice melt from human-caused warming |

## 8. Milankovitch (Orbital) Cycles

> **Definition**: **Milankovitch cycles** are long-term cyclical variations in Earth's orbital parameters (proposed by Serbian scientist Milutin Milankovitch) that alter the amount and distribution of solar radiation reaching Earth — considered the primary driver of ice age cycles.

**Three Orbital Variations**:

| Cycle | What Changes | Period | Climate Effect |
|-------|-------------|--------|----------------|
| **Eccentricity** | Shape of orbit: nearly circular ↔ slightly elliptical | ~100,000 yrs | Varies distance from Sun; affects seasonal energy differences; currently becoming more circular |
| **Obliquity** | Axial tilt: 22.1° ↔ 24.5° | ~41,000 yrs | Greater tilt → more extreme seasons; favors **deglaciation** at higher latitudes |
| **Precession** | Wobble of Earth's rotational axis | ~26,000 yrs | Alters which hemisphere/season gets more solar radiation |

**Combined Effect**: Variations of up to **25%** in incoming solar radiation (insolation) at mid-latitudes

**Current Status**:
- Eccentricity: very slowly decreasing (approaching most circular)
- Obliquity: currently at 23.4° — slowly decreasing (last max ~10,000 yrs ago)
- Decreasing obliquity → milder seasons → warmer winters, cooler summers → ice builds up at high latitudes
