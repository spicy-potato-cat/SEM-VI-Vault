## 1. Heat Transport

> **Definition**: **Heat transport** is the movement of thermal energy from one region to another due to temperature differences — in the climate system this occurs via conduction, convection, and radiation.

**Three Modes**:

| Mode | Description | Climate Example |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Conduction** | Heat transfer through direct contact (no mass movement) | Surface heating lowest air layer |
| **Convection** | Heat transfer via movement of fluid masses | Rising warm air creating thunderstorms |
| **Radiation** | Energy transfer via electromagnetic waves | Solar radiation, Earth's longwave emission |

**Sensible Heat Flux**: Surface warms air layer by conduction → warm air rises (thermal convection) → carries heat high into atmosphere.

## 2. Coriolis Effect

> **Definition**: **Coriolis acceleration** is an apparent force caused by Earth's rotation that deflects moving objects (air, water, projectiles) to the **right** in the Northern Hemisphere and to the **left** in the Southern Hemisphere.

**Key Properties**:
- Perpendicular to the object's direction of motion
- At the **Equator**: Coriolis force = **ZERO**
- Increases in strength toward the poles
- Determined by object's speed, mass, and latitude

**Why cyclones don't form at the Equator (CT-II Set 2, Q1)**:
- Cyclonic rotation requires the Coriolis force to initiate and sustain spiral motion
- At equator, Coriolis = 0 → no deflection → air flows directly to pressure centers → no spin
- Tropical cyclones form at **≥5° latitude** where Coriolis is sufficient

**Cyclone rotation**:
- Northern Hemisphere → **counterclockwise** (low pressure)
- Southern Hemisphere → **clockwise** (low pressure)

## 3. Geostrophic Motion

> **Definition**: **Geostrophic motion** is fluid flow **parallel to lines of equal pressure (isobars)** in a rotating system, resulting from the balance between the pressure-gradient force and the Coriolis force (in low-friction conditions).

**Mechanism**:
```
Normal expectation: Air flows High Pressure → Low Pressure (pressure gradient)
Reality (upper atmosphere, >2 km):
    Air starts moving toward low pressure
         ↓ Coriolis deflects it (right in N. Hemisphere)
    Air deflects until flowing PARALLEL to isobars
         = Geostrophic balance
```

**Why it matters**: Upper-level winds (jet streams, etc.) are approximately geostrophic — understanding them is key to weather forecasting.

## 4. Global Wind Systems

**Permanent (Planetary) Winds**:

| Wind | Direction | Latitude | Characteristics |
|------|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Trade Winds** | East → West (Easterlies) | 30°N to 30°S | Consistent, reliable; drive tropical ocean currents |
| **Westerlies** | West → East | 30°–60° N&S | Mid-latitude weather systems; jet streams |
| **Polar Easterlies** | East → West | >60° N&S | Cold, dry, dense air descending from polar highs |

**Highs and Lows**:
- **High Pressure (Anticyclone)**: Air descends → diverges outward → dry, clear, stable weather
- **Low Pressure (Cyclone)**: Air converges → rises → clouds, rain, storms

## 5. Hadley Cell

> **Definition**: The **Hadley Cell** is a large-scale atmospheric circulation pattern (proposed by George Hadley, 1735) in which warm equatorial air rises, flows poleward aloft, descends at ~30° latitude, and returns to the equator near the surface as Trade Winds.

**Mechanism**:
```
        POLE ←──── HIGH ALTITUDE (poleward flow, cools) ────→ POLE
                                      ↓
                      Subtropical High (~30°N / 30°S)
                         Dry; desert belts (Sahara, etc.)
                                      ↓
    ◄──────── SURFACE (Trade Winds, equatorward flow) ──────────►
                                      ↑
              EQUATOR — Warm, moist air RISES — ITCZ
              (Intertropical Convergence Zone — heavy rainfall)
```

**Key Outputs**:
- **Trade Winds** = surface return flow of Hadley Cell (blow equatorward)
- **ITCZ** = zone of rising air, heavy precipitation
- **Subtropical Deserts** = zone of descending dry air (~30° latitude)

## 6. Monsoon Rains

> **Definition**: **Monsoon** is a seasonal reversal of wind direction causing a distinct wet and dry season; the Indian Summer Monsoon brings ~75–90% of India's annual rainfall.

**Indian Summer Monsoon**:
- Season: **June–September** (4 months)
- Western and central India: receive **>90%** of total annual precipitation in this period
- Southern and northwestern India: 50–75% of annual rainfall
- **Mechanism**: Ocean evaporation → moist air rises → condenses → heavy rainfall over land
- Wind classification:
  - **Southwest (Summer) Monsoon**: warm, moist; from Indian Ocean toward land
  - **Northeast (Winter) Monsoon**: dry retreating winds; rainfall only on southeast coast

## 7. Cloud Formation and Climate

**Cloud Formation Process**:
```
Moist air rises → air cools with altitude → reaches dew point →
water vapour condenses on tiny particles → cloud droplets form
(same process at ground level = fog)
```

**Climate Role of Clouds**:
- Regulate solar energy reaching Earth's surface
- If skies **clear**: heat escapes to space → cooler surface temperatures
- If **cloudy**: some outgoing heat re-absorbed → warmer surface

**Climate Change effects on clouds**:
- Warmer Earth → **elevates clouds** (troposphere extends higher)
- Warming also **moves clouds poleward** (circulation patterns in tropics expanding)

## 8. Hurricanes and Global Warming

> **Definition**: **Hurricanes** (= typhoons in NW Pacific, tropical cyclones generally) are rapidly rotating low-pressure systems with thunder, lightning, and strong winds that develop in tropical/subtropical oceans.

**Climate Change Influences on Hurricanes**:
- **Warmer sea surface temperatures**: more energy available → stronger storms (higher wind speeds, more rainfall)
- **Sea level rise**: storm surges reach further inland → greater flooding
- **Changed intensity distribution**: fewer but more intense storms expected

## 9. Ice-Albedo Feedback

> **Definition**: **Ice-albedo feedback** is a **positive feedback** where a decrease in ice/snow cover lowers Earth's albedo (reflectivity), causing more solar energy to be absorbed, which raises temperature, melting more ice — amplifying the initial warming.

> **Definition**: **Albedo** is the fraction of incoming solar radiation reflected by a surface or body (0 = perfect absorber, 1 = perfect reflector).

**Surface Albedo Values**:

| Surface | Albedo | Effect |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Fresh snow / ice | 0.80–0.90 | Reflects most solar radiation |
| Ocean | 0.06 | Absorbs most solar radiation |
| Forest | 0.10–0.20 | Moderate absorption |
| Desert (sand) | 0.25–0.40 | Moderate reflection |
| Earth average | ~0.30 | 30% of solar radiation reflected |

**Engineering Applications — 3 Albedo-Based Cooling Techniques (PYQ Q3b)**:
1. **Cool Roof Technologies** — white/reflective roofing to reduce urban heat absorption in buildings
2. **Marine Cloud Brightening (MCB)** — seeding sea salt particles into marine clouds to make them more reflective
3. **Ground-level Albedo Enhancement** — whitening roads, reflective crops, urban pavements

## 10. Water Vapour Feedback

> **Definition**: **Water vapour feedback** is the process where increased temperature → increased evaporation → more atmospheric water vapour (itself a GHG) → more heat trapped → further warming. It is the **strongest positive feedback** in the climate system.

**Key Facts**:
- Water vapour = **most abundant greenhouse gas**
- Amplifies warming from CO₂ and other GHGs approximately **2–3×**
- Secondary effects: more water vapour → more clouds + precipitation (complex outcomes)
- This is why the atmosphere is 33°C warmer than the naked planet model predicts

---

## 11. Aerosols

> **Definition**: **Aerosols** are tiny liquid droplets or solid particles suspended in the atmosphere — including dust, soot (black carbon), sea salt, sulfate particles, and volcanic ash. They affect climate by interacting with radiation and clouds.

**Two Types of Aerosol Effects**:

| Effect | Mechanism | Net Result |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| **Direct** | Sulfate aerosols scatter incoming solar radiation | **Cooling** (regional/global) |
| **Direct (soot)** | Black carbon absorbs solar radiation | **Warming** |
| **Indirect (cloud effect)** | Aerosols act as cloud condensation nuclei → more, smaller droplets → brighter clouds → more reflection | **Cooling** |

**Differentiating Climate and Weather (PYQ Q4b)**:

| Feature | **Weather** | **Climate** |
|---------|------------|------------|
| Timescale | Short-term (hours to days) | Long-term (30+ years) |
| Variability | High variability | Statistical averages and trends |
| Example | "It rained in Mumbai today" | "Mumbai receives 2400 mm annually" |
| Driving forces | Synoptic pressure systems | Radiative balance, ocean-atmosphere coupling |

## 12. Global Ocean Circulation

> **Definition**: **Thermohaline circulation** is the global ocean circulation pattern driven by differences in water **temperature (thermo)** and **salinity (haline)**, forming the "global conveyor belt" of deep ocean currents.

**Five Major Ocean Gyres**: North Atlantic, South Atlantic, North Pacific, South Pacific, Indian Ocean Gyre

**Gulf Stream**:
- Formed from: convergence of North Atlantic Equatorial Current + Florida Current
- Speed: avg **6.4 km/hr**, max ~**9 km/hr** (fastest ocean current)
- Transports: **>100×** the combined flow of all Earth's rivers
- Role: transports tropical heat northward; keeps **Western Europe warm**
- Western intensification: narrow (50–100 km wide), deep (to 1.5 km), fast

**The Deep Atlantic Conveyor (AMOC)**:
- Stable for ~**8,000 years** — constant companion for human civilization
- Warm surface water flows north → cools → becomes dense → sinks in North Atlantic → flows south as deep cold water
- Drives heat redistribution globally
- **Threat from climate change**: Polar warming → surface water not as cold/dense → weakened circulation; Greenland meltwater (freshwater) → lowers salinity → reduces sinking → further weakening

**Deep Ocean Circulation Diagram**:
```
Surface warm water flows NORTH (Gulf Stream) →
        ↓ Cools in North Atlantic
        ↓ Becomes dense (cold + salty) → SINKS
        ↓ Deep cold water flows SOUTH and into other oceans
        ↓ Slowly warms and upwells elsewhere
        ↓ Returns to surface → flows back North
            (cycle takes ~1,000 years)
```

## 13. El Niño and La Niña (ENSO)

> **Definition**: **El Niño–Southern Oscillation (ENSO)** is a periodic climate pattern involving coupled ocean-atmosphere variability in the tropical Pacific Ocean — alternating between warm (El Niño) and cool (La Niña) phases.

| Condition | Ocean Surface Temp | East Winds | Key Effects |
|-----------|-------------------|------------|-------------|
| **El Niño** | Warmer than average | Weaker | Increased rainfall/flooding in East Pacific; drought in Australia/SE Asia; disrupts fisheries |
| **La Niña** | Cooler than normal | Stronger | Opposite effects — drought in S. America; more rain in Australia/SE Asia |
| Neutral | Average | Normal | Standard weather patterns |

**Frequency**: Typically every **3–5 years**

**Climate Change Link**: ENSO patterns projected to become more intense and potentially more frequent with global warming.

## 14. Global Ice Sheet Degradation

> **Definition**: **Global ice sheet degradation** is the ongoing loss of ice from polar ice sheets (Greenland and Antarctica) due to surface warming, ocean warming, and changing wind/current patterns.

**Causes**:
- Shifting winds and ocean currents pushing warm waters toward East Antarctica
- Westerly winds thrusting warm waters toward East Antarctic ice sheet
- Direct atmospheric warming from rising GHG concentrations

**Consequences**:
- Meltwater responsible for ~**⅓ of global sea level rise** since 1993
- East Antarctic ice has thinned at alarming rates in recent decades
- Positive feedback: ice loss → less albedo → more heat absorbed → more ice loss
