## 3. CS1 — Great Barrier Reef (Biodiversity)

**Location**: Coral Sea, off Queensland coast, northeastern Australia
**Key Issue**: Marine biodiversity loss from coral bleaching and ocean acidification

**Background**:
> The Great Barrier Reef is the world's **largest coral reef system** (~2,300 km long), home to thousands of species of fish, corals, marine turtles, and other organisms. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

**Climate Impacts**:

| Impact | Mechanism | Consequence |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Coral Bleaching** | Rising sea surface temperatures → coral expel symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) → lose nutrients and color → death | Mass die-offs of coral; loss of habitat for thousands of species |
| **Ocean Acidification** | Oceans absorb more CO₂ → carbonic acid formation → lower pH → weakens coral skeletons and shells | Slower coral growth, structural collapse; molluscs and crustaceans also affected |
| **Increased Storm Intensity** | Warmer ocean = more energy for cyclones | Physical damage to reef structures; sediment smothering corals |

**Consequences**:
- **Biodiversity loss**: coral provides habitat for 25% of all marine species
- **Disrupted food chains**: collapse of reef → cascading effects on fishing and marine ecosystems
- **Loss of coastal protection**: healthy reefs reduce wave energy; degraded reefs leave coastlines vulnerable
- **Economic loss**: tourism (>AUD 6.4 billion/year) and fishing industries at risk

**Adaptation and Management Responses**:
- Marine Protected Areas (MPAs): limiting human activity in reef zones
- Coral restoration techniques: **coral gardening** (growing coral fragments in nurseries and transplanting), **assisted evolution** (selective breeding for heat tolerance)
- Reducing local stressors: water quality improvement, limiting fishing and runoff
- Global: reducing carbon emissions to limit temperature rise

**Lessons Learned**:
- Local management is insufficient without global emission reductions
- Ecosystem resilience can be improved with active restoration
- Biodiversity loss has cascading social-economic effects

## 4. CS2 — Sahel Region, Africa (Food Production and Livelihoods)

> 📌 **HIGH PRIORITY** — Maps to PYQ Q7b "case study food security + food production"

**Location**: Semi-arid belt stretching south of the Sahara Desert, across West and Central Africa (Mauritania, Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Chad, Sudan, etc.)
**Key Issue**: Climate change-driven food production collapse and livelihood destruction

**Background**:
> The Sahel is one of the world's most climate-vulnerable regions — chronically low and highly unreliable rainfall, limited agricultural infrastructure, widespread poverty, and ongoing conflict make it extremely susceptible.

**Climate-Specific Challenges**:

| Challenge | Impact |
|-----------|--------|
| **Unreliable and Decreasing Rainfall** | Crop failures; growing season shortening |
| **Increased Drought Frequency** | Severe water stress; desertification expanding |
| **Temperature Extremes** | Heat stress on livestock and crops |
| **Land Degradation** | Overgrazing × drought = soil erosion, desertification |
| **Flooding (when it rains)** | Intense rainfall events wash away topsoil and crops |

**Food Production Impacts**:
- **Crop failure**: reduced and unreliable rainfall combined with extreme heat events → crops fail regularly
- **Land degradation**: overgrazing + drought → loss of topsoil + desertification → permanently reduced agricultural land
- **Livestock loss**: mass deaths in prolonged droughts → reduced dietary diversity, loss of income for pastoralist communities

**Livelihood Impacts**:
- Reduced agricultural income → poverty deepens
- Mass displacement and forced migration of farming communities
- Conflict over remaining water and arable land
- Women and children disproportionately affected (water collection, food preparation)

**Adaptation Responses** (in Sahel):
- Farmer-managed natural regeneration (FMNR) — protecting and managing native trees within agricultural fields
- Improved water harvesting (half-moon pits, contour stone bunds)
- Drought-resistant crop varieties
- Community-based seed banks

**Lessons Learned**:
- Climate adaptation must be integrated with poverty reduction and conflict resolution
- Traditional farming knowledge (indigenous practices) is valuable for adaptation
- Success requires community ownership and women's empowerment

## 5. CS3 — Eastern Africa (Food Security)

> 📌 **HIGH PRIORITY** — PYQ Q7b "food security and food production"

**Location**: Kenya, Ethiopia, Tanzania, Uganda (called the "Breadbasket of Africa" for regional grain production)
**Key Issue**: Multiple climate stressors undermining food security

**Climate Impacts**:

| Impact | Mechanism | Consequence |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Droughts and Floods** | More frequent and severe extreme events | Crop failure, livestock death, infrastructure damage |
| **Water Scarcity** | Glacier retreat in Kilimanjaro and Ethiopian Highlands + changing precipitation | Reduced irrigation water availability; pastoralist conflict |
| **Pests and Diseases** | Warmer, more humid conditions favor locusts, Fall Armyworm, crop pathogens | Significant crop losses (e.g., desert locust crisis 2019–2021) |
| **Sea Level Rise** | Coastal land loss, saltwater intrusion | Salinization of coastal agricultural land in Kenya, Tanzania |

**Food Security Consequences**:
- **Reduced food availability**: crop failures and livestock deaths → less food in markets
- **Higher food prices** → malnutrition especially in children
- **Population displacement**: farmers forced to leave agricultural land
- **Conflict**: competition for water and agricultural land between communities

**Adaptation Responses**:
- **Drought-resistant crop varieties** (orange-fleshed sweet potato, drought-tolerant maize)
- **Water-saving irrigation**: drip irrigation, rainwater harvesting
- **Crop diversification**: multiple crops to reduce monoculture risk
- **Climate-resilient infrastructure**: drought/flood forecasting, rural roads to maintain supply chains
- **International cooperation**: IGAD (Intergovernmental Authority on Development) early warning systems

**Lessons Learned**:
- Food system resilience requires simultaneous action on production, storage, distribution, and access
- Early warning systems save lives and livelihoods if combined with timely response
- Regional cooperation essential for transboundary challenges (locust control, river basin management)

## 6. CS4 — Chennai Heatwaves, India (Health)

**Location**: Chennai, Tamil Nadu, India
**Key Issue**: Escalating heat extremes threatening human health and economic productivity

**Background**:
> Chennai sits near sea level on the southeastern coast of India. Its climate is tropical but increasingly extreme — the May 2023 heat event was the most intense on record.

**Key Data Point**: May 2023 — hottest recorded day in Chennai: **45.6°C (114°F)**

**Direct Health Impacts**:

| Health Impact | Who is Vulnerable |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Heatstroke, dehydration, heat exhaustion | Outdoor workers, elderly, children |
| Hospital surge capacity overwhelmed | All populations during peak events |
| Heat-related fatalities | Most severe for elderly and urban poor |

**Indirect Health Impacts**:
- **Air quality**: extreme heat worsens ground-level ozone and pollution → respiratory illness (asthma, COPD)
- **Water scarcity**: heat dries up urban water sources → waterborne diseases (diarrhea, cholera)
- **Mental health**: chronic stress, displacement anxiety

**Economic Impacts**:
- **Construction workers** cannot work outdoors safely → significant productivity loss
- **Agricultural productivity** suffers from heat stress on crops and livestock
- **Energy demand** spikes with cooling needs → power grid strain → blackouts

**Adaptation Responses**:
- **Urban tree planting** (including Miyawaki forests) to reduce urban heat island effect
- **Cool roof technologies**: reflective roofing materials on buildings to reduce heat absorption
- **Public awareness campaigns**: heatstroke prevention, hydration advisories
- **Early warning systems**: government alerts and heat action plans (Ahmedabad model)
- **Cooling centers**: public spaces with air conditioning for vulnerable populations

**Lessons Learned**:
- Urban heat islands amplify general warming → cities need specific adaptation strategies
- Heat action plans (early warning + cooling access) are cost-effective life-saving tools
- Tree cover and green spaces are multi-benefit urban adaptation (cooling, biodiversity, water absorption)

## 7. CS5 — Xua Lai Village, Vietnam (Livelihoods and Poverty)

**Location**: Xua Lai, small coastal village, Vietnam
**Key Issue**: Climate change destroying shrimp farming livelihoods and deepening poverty

**Background**:
> Xua Lai is a typical small coastal community in Vietnam relying almost entirely on **shrimp aquaculture** for income and food security. The entire socioeconomic structure of the village depends on this single coastal activity.

**Climate Impacts**:

| Impact | Mechanism | Effect |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Salinity Intrusion** | Sea level rise + reduced freshwater flow → saltwater invades ponds | Disease outbreaks (shrimp die-off); reduced yields |
| **Sea Level Rise** | Coastal erosion, saltwater flooding of low-lying ponds | Physical damage to ponds; habitat loss |
| **Extreme Weather Events** | More intense typhoons; irregular rainfall | Damage to ponds, infrastructure; disrupts harvest cycle |
| **Temperature Rise** | Warmer water → lower dissolved oxygen → stress on shrimp | Reduced growth, higher mortality |

**Livelihood Impacts**:
- Shrimp yields significantly reduced → reduced income
- Job losses in harvesting, processing, distribution
- Food insecurity: people cannot afford food because of both low income AND high food prices

**Poverty Deepening**:
- Lack of financial resources to invest in adaptation technologies
- Debt accumulation to survive → displacement of families
- **Women especially vulnerable**: primary caregivers; often excluded from adaptation resources
- **Children leave school**: families need additional income; education disrupted

**Adaptation Responses**:
- **Salt-tolerant shrimp varieties**: new species that can survive higher-salinity water
- **Improved pond management**: water quality monitoring, better drainage systems
- **Community seawalls and embankments**: basic coastal protection
- **Diversification**: some households shifting to rice or vegetable farming
- **Advocacy**: NGOs supporting communities in securing government support for climate compensation

**Lessons Learned**:
- Communities dependent on a single climate-sensitive resource are most vulnerable
- Gender-sensitive adaptation is essential — women need targeted support and inclusion
- External climate finance must reach the local level effectively (not just national governments)
- Even low-cost adaptation measures (salt-tolerant varieties, pond management) can significantly improve outcomes
