## 1. Climate Mitigation — Targets and Goals

> **Definition**: **Climate change mitigation** involves actions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions or enhance carbon sinks to limit the magnitude of global warming.

**Key Targets**:

| Target | Value |
|--------|-------|
| IPCC mitigation goal | "Stabilize GHG levels sufficient for ecosystems to adapt, food production not threatened, economic development sustainable" |
| Paris Agreement | **Well below 2°C**; pursue **1.5°C** above pre-industrial levels |
| Net zero target | By approximately **2050** |
| CO₂ milestone | First exceeded **400 ppm** in **2013** — first time in human history |
| 2024 CO₂ level | ~**420 ppm** |

## 2. Low Carbon Development

> **Definition**: **Low Carbon Development (LCD)** (also called green growth or Low Emission Development Strategies, LEDS) is a development pathway that achieves economic growth while minimizing GHG emissions.

**Low-Carbon Energy Sources**:

| Energy Source | Type | Notes |
|--------------|------|-------|
| **Solar** | Renewable | Fastest growing; declining costs |
| **Wind** | Renewable | Offshore + onshore; high growth in India |
| **Geothermal** | Renewable | Stable baseload; limited geography |
| **Hydropower** | Renewable | Large installed base; some ecological trade-offs |
| **Tidal / Wave** | Renewable | Emerging; high potential in coastal areas |
| **Nuclear** | Low-carbon (not renewable) | High capital cost; zero operational GHG emissions |

**Benefits of LCD**:
- Cleaner air (co-benefit: reduces PM₂.₅, NOₓ pollution)
- Cost savings (energy efficiency + lower fuel costs)
- Energy independence and security
- Job creation in new sectors

**Requirements**: Integrated planning that combines development priorities with climate priorities; carbon pricing signals to guide investment.

## 3. CCS — CO₂ Capture and Sequestration

> **Definition**: **CCS (Carbon Capture and Sequestration)** is the process of capturing CO₂ at large point sources (power plants, industrial facilities), compressing it, transporting it, and injecting it into deep underground geological formations for long-term storage.

**Process**:
```
CO₂ Source (power plant, industrial facility)
         ↓  Capture at point of emission
  Compression (liquefied CO₂ — very high pressure)
         ↓
  Transport via specially designed CO₂ pipelines
         ↓
  Injection into deep underground porous rock formations
  (saline aquifers, depleted oil/gas fields, coal seams)
         ↓
  Permanent geological storage underground
```

> 📌 **Carbon capture ≠ Carbon sequestration**:
> - **Capture**: removing CO₂ from emission sources or the atmosphere
> - **Sequestration**: long-term storage of captured CO₂

**Commercial CCS Applications**:
- Coal gasification (Integrated Gasification Combined Cycle — IGCC)
- Ethanol production (fermentation captures CO₂ easily)
- Fertilizer production (CO₂ byproduct from ammonia synthesis)
- Natural gas processing (CO₂ separated to meet pipeline spec)
- Coal-fired power generation (post-combustion capture)

**Limitations**:
- Requires **≥10% additional energy** diversion for coal plants to power the capture process
- High capital and operating costs
- Unknown long-term storage integrity (potential leakage)
- Special CO₂ pipelines needed for transport
- Not a substitute for emission reductions — a supplement

## 4. Types of Carbon Sequestration

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Biological** | CO₂ stored in living vegetation and soils through photosynthesis | Forests, grasslands, wetlands, oceans, crop soils |
| **Geological** | CO₂ captured and injected into underground porous rock formations; pressurized to liquid | Deep saline aquifers, depleted oil/gas fields |
| **Technological** | Direct Air Capture (DAC) — extracts CO₂ directly from the atmosphere using chemical sorbents | DAC plants (Climeworks, Carbon Engineering); enhanced weathering |

**Direct Air Capture (DAC)**:
- Large fans pull air through chemical filters that bind CO₂
- Very energy-intensive; current cost ~$300–$600/tonne CO₂
- Scales independently of emission sources — can locate anywhere
- Critical for achieving net-negative emissions post-2050

## 5. Carbon Sinks

> **Definition**: A **carbon sink** is any natural or artificial system that absorbs more carbon from the atmosphere than it releases — storing it in long-term reservoirs.

**Natural Carbon Sinks**:

| Sink | Mechanism |
|------|-----------|
| **Forests** | Photosynthesis; biomass accumulation; soil organic matter |
| **Oceans** | Physical dissolution of CO₂; biological pump (marine organisms) |
| **Soils / Grasslands** | Soil organic carbon accumulation |
| **Wetlands and Mangroves ("Blue Carbon")** | Highly efficient; store carbon in waterlogged soils for millennia |

> 📌 **Deforestation** = converts a carbon sink into a **carbon source** (biomass burned/decays → CO₂ released). Forest regrowth and afforestation = carbon sequestration.

**Benefits Beyond Carbon Storage** (co-benefits of forests):
- Provides: mushrooms, maple syrup, honey, berries, medicinal plants, timber, watershed protection, biodiversity habitat

---

## 6. Carbon Sequestration Station

> **Definition**: A **Carbon Sequestration Station** is a facility or natural area specifically designed and managed to capture and store CO₂ — including both natural (forest plots, wetlands) and industrial installations.

**Types**:
- **Natural Stations**: managed forests, wetlands, mangrove restoration areas
- **Industrial Stations**: coal gasification plants with CCS, ethanol production facilities, natural gas processing plants, fertilizer plants

**Why it matters**: Complements emission reduction — even with aggressive mitigation, we need to actively remove CO₂ from the atmosphere to meet Paris Agreement targets.

## 7. Miyawaki Method

> 📌 **HIGH PRIORITY** — Frequently tested; covers both Unit 7 (mitigation) and Unit 5 (adaptation)

> **Definition**: The **Miyawaki Method** is a dense, rapid urban afforestation technique developed by Japanese botanist **Akira Miyawaki**, using native multi-layered plant species to create self-sustaining forests in 20–30 years that would take 200 years naturally.

**Step-by-Step Process**:
```
STEP 1: Soil Preparation
  → Loosen compacted urban soil
  → Add organic matter: rice husks, coconut shells, vermicompost

STEP 2: Native Species Selection
  → Choose 30–50+ species from ALL layers:
    Ground cover → Shrubs → Sub-trees → Trees → Canopy
  → ONLY native species — matched to local ecosystem

STEP 3: Dense Planting
  → Plant 3–5 plants per m² (much denser than conventional)
  → Mixed species planted together (no monoculture)

STEP 4: Active Management (first 2–3 years)
  → Regular watering and weeding for first 2–3 years
  → No fertilizers or pesticides

STEP 5: Self-Sustaining (after year 3)
  → Forest becomes self-sustaining: no further intervention needed
  → Leaf litter creates its own mulch layer
  → Biodiversity colonizes naturally
```

**Key Features and Outcomes**:

| Feature | Value |
|---------|-------|
| Growth speed | **10× faster** than conventional forest |
| Density | **30× denser** than conventional forest |
| CO₂ absorption | **30× more** CO₂ than monoculture plantation |
| Time to forest | **20–30 years** (vs. 200 years naturally) |
| Self-sustaining | After **2–3 years** (no water, fertilizer, pesticide) |
| Maintenance cost | Low after initial phase |
| Species type | **Native species ONLY** |
| Land required | Works even on small urban plots (100 m² minimum) |

**Dual Role**:
- **Adaptation**: reduces urban heat island, flood absorption, air quality improvement, biodiversity support
- **Mitigation**: significant CO₂ sequestration; substitutes fossil-fuel based urban cooling

**India Examples**:
- **Mumbai**: Aarey Colony — multiple Miyawaki forest plots
- **Hyderabad**: Municipal-scale plantations
- **Delhi**: Urban green belt initiatives
- **NMMC (Navi Mumbai Municipal Corporation)**: 100+ Miyawaki forests planted

## 8. Solar Radiation Management (SRM / Geoengineering)

> **Definition**: **Solar Radiation Management (SRM)** (also called solar geoengineering or albedo modification) is a proposed category of intervention to reflect a small amount of incoming solar energy back to space, thereby reducing surface temperatures without reducing GHG concentrations.

**Key Proposals**:

| Technique | Mechanism |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Stratospheric Aerosol Injection (SAI)** | Releasing sulfate aerosols in the stratosphere → reflects incoming solar radiation (mimics volcanic winter effect) |
| **Marine Cloud Brightening (MCB)** | Spraying sea salt into low marine clouds → increases cloud droplet number → brighter, more reflective clouds |
| **Ground-Level Albedo Enhancement** | Whitening roads, rooftops; reflective crops; painting surfaces white |

**Critical Limitations**:
- Does **NOT reduce GHG concentrations**
- Does **NOT address ocean acidification** (CO₂ still dissolves in oceans)
- **Termination shock**: stopping SRM suddenly causes rapid warming
- Governance challenges: unilateral deployment could affect other countries' weather patterns
- Unknown side effects on precipitation, regional weather patterns

## 9. Biofuels

> **Definition**: **Biofuels** are fuels derived from **living plants** or organic material (biomass) — considered carbon-neutral because CO₂ emitted during combustion was recently absorbed from the atmosphere by the plant.

**Types of Biofuels**:

| Type | Source | GHG Reduction vs. Petrol |
|------|--------|--------------------------|
| **Bioethanol (E10/E20/E22)** | Sugarcane, corn (grain fermentation) | ~20–30% |
| **Biodiesel** | Vegetable oils, animal fats (transesterification) | ~40–60% |
| **Cellulosic Ethanol** | Agricultural waste, grasses, wood (lignocellulosic material) | Up to **86%** |

**Why Biofuels are "Carbon-Neutral"** — 3-Way Reduction:
1. **Avoids gasoline/diesel combustion emissions** (replaces fossil fuel)
2. **Keeps fossil carbon in storage** (less oil/gas burned)
3. **Grows new biomass** that absorbs CO₂ from the atmosphere again

**Advantages**:
- Significant GHG reduction potential
- Reduces fossil fuel import dependence
- Rural income and job creation
- Liquid fuel (easy to use in existing engines)

**Disadvantages**:

| Disadvantage | Explanation |
|-------------|-------------|
| High cost | Production still more expensive than fossil fuels |
| Food security risk | Crops used for fuel rather than food → higher food prices |
| Land use change | New land cleared → deforestation (negates carbon benefit) |
| Water use | High irrigation demand for energy crops |
| Monoculture risk | Large plantations of single crop → biodiversity loss |
| Fertilizer use | Synthetic fertilizers generate N₂O (300× GWP of CO₂) |
| Industrial pollution | Biorefinery waste water and emissions |

## 10. Gas Hydrates

> **Definition**: **Gas hydrates** (methane clathrates) are crystalline solid compounds consisting of a **water cage structure (lattice) that traps a methane gas molecule** inside. Stable at high pressure and temperatures above 0°C — found in seafloor sediments and permafrost.

**Why They Matter for Climate**:
- Estimated to contain **~2× more carbon** than all known fossil fuel deposits combined
- Climate warming destabilizes hydrates in three ways:
  1. Temperature rise → lattice breaks apart → methane released
  2. Pressure drop (sea level fall) → hydrate stability zone shifts
  3. Seabed warming from ocean temperature rise

**Where Found**:
- Deep freshwater lakes (e.g., Baikal)
- **Siberia and Alaska** (permafrost regions)
- **Oceanic continental shelves** (deep seabed)

**Climate Feedback Loop**:
```
Warming → Hydrate destabilization → Methane released (CH₄ = 25× CO₂)
  → Stronger greenhouse effect → More warming → More hydrate release
```

> 📌 This is a potential **tipping point** — large-scale hydrate release could cause irreversible runaway warming.

## 11. Social Cost of Carbon (SCC)

> **Definition**: The **Social Cost of Carbon (SCC)** is the estimated economic damage caused by emitting one additional tonne of CO₂ into the atmosphere — expressed in monetary terms.

**What SCC Includes**:
- Reduced agricultural productivity
- Healthcare costs from heat, air quality, vector-borne diseases
- Property damage from extreme weather events
- Energy system adjustment costs
- Loss of ecosystem services

**Global SCC Values**:

| Country / Body | SCC (USD/tonne CO₂) |
|---------------|---------------------|
| **India** | **$86** (highest nationally) |
| **USA** | $48 (Trump-era): Biden proposed **$190** |
| **Global Social Average** | ~$31 (many estimates vary widely) |

**Challenges with SCC**:
- High uncertainty in climate models, economic models, and discount rates
- Does not capture all damages (biodiversity loss, mass migration, cultural losses)
- Discount rate choice dramatically affects value (intergenerational equity debate)

## 12. Short and Long-Term Mitigation Strategies

**Immediate (Short-Term) Actions**:

| Strategy | Impact |
|----------|--------|
| **Put a price on carbon** | Makes high-emission activities more expensive; incentivizes clean alternatives |
| **End fossil fuel subsidies** | Removes market distortion ($5.9 trillion/year global fossil fuel subsidies per IMF estimate) |
| **Energy efficiency** | Buildings, industry, transport — largest near-term potential |
| **Renewable energy** | Solar and wind rapidly becoming cheapest electricity sources |

**Long-Term (Structural) Actions**:

| Strategy | Impact |
|----------|--------|
| **CCS deployment at scale** | Allows remaining fossil use while capturing emissions |
| **Low-carbon cities** | Integrated planning (transit, building codes, urban forests) |
| **Climate-smart agriculture** | Reduce N₂O and CH₄ from food systems; carbon farming |
| **Forest landscape restoration** | Protect and restore forests for carbon storage + biodiversity |
| **Technology innovation + transfer** | Advanced nuclear, green hydrogen, direct air capture |
| **GEF support** | Global Environment Facility funding for technology transfer to developing nations |

**Three Policy Instruments** (from Unit 5):
1. **Carbon Pricing** (tax or ETS)
2. **Technology Subsidies** (feed-in tariffs, R&D funding)
3. **Performance Standards** (fuel economy, appliance efficiency)

## 13. Forest Cover in India

> 📌 **HIGH PRIORITY** — Important factual data for exam

> **Definition**: **Forest Cover** refers to all lands with a tree canopy density of more than 10% and an area of more than one hectare, irrespective of ownership or legal status.

**India Forest Cover Statistics** (Forest Survey of India Report 2021):

| Category | Area (sq km) | % of India's Geography |
|----------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Very Dense Forest** (>70% canopy) | 99,779 | 3.04% |
| **Moderately Dense Forest** (40–70%) | 3,06,890 | 9.33% |
| **Open Forest** (10–40%) | 3,07,120 | 9.34% |
| **Total Forest Cover** | **7,13,789** | **21.71%** |
| **Tree Cover** (outside forests) | 95,748 | 2.91% |
| **Total Forest + Tree Cover** | **8,09,537** | **24.62%** |

**Targets and Policy**:
- **National Forest Policy 1988**: goal of **33% forest/tree cover** of India's geographic area
- Current deficit: India at ~24.62% vs. 33% target — **8.4% gap** to close

**Top States by Forest Area**:
1. Madhya Pradesh (largest forest area)
2. Arunachal Pradesh
3. Chhattisgarh

**Top States by Forest % of State Area**:
1. **Mizoram** (~84%)
2. Arunachal Pradesh
3. Meghalaya

**Change 2019 → 2021**: Net increase of **2,261 sq km** in forest and tree cover

**Climate Role**:
- India's forests absorb **~15%** of India's total CO₂ emissions annually
- India's NDC: create **2.5–3 billion tonnes** additional CO₂ equivalent carbon sink by 2030

**Compensatory Afforestation**: **CAMPA (Compensatory Afforestation Fund Management and Planning Authority)** — fund for afforestation when forests are diverted for development.

## 14. India's Climate Performance

**CCPI 2023** (Climate Change Performance Index):
- India rank: **8th** (up from 10th in 2022) — GOOD
- High performance: all major categories
- Medium rating: renewable energy (still catching up)
- Top performers globally: Denmark (1st), Sweden (2nd), Chile (3rd)
- Among G20: only **India, UK, and the EU** ranked as high performers

**EPI 2022** (Environmental Performance Index):
- India rank: **180th out of 180** (LAST)
- Score: 18.9 (very low)
- Key problem areas: air quality (PM₂.₅), GHG emission trends, biodiversity, rule of law

> 📌 Apparent contradiction: India is a **climate high performer (CCPI)** but an **environmental low performer (EPI)** — because CCPI measures climate policy and mitigation effort, while EPI measures environmental health outcomes and air/water quality — both are real.
